How to budget a remodel without fooling yourself

Most remodel budgets fail the same way: they're built on the finished photo, not the work behind the walls. Here's how we tell clients to build one that holds.

Start with the range, not the wish. In Portland right now, a mid-range kitchen runs $45–90k, a hall bath $25–55k, an addition $90–180k. If a number you've heard is far below these, something is missing from it — usually demolition, disposal, electrical, or drywall repair in the rooms next door.

Hold back 10–15% as contingency. Not for upgrades — for what the walls hide. In houses older than 1960, we find something unplanned on roughly half of jobs: dry rot, undersized wiring, a pipe run nobody would approve today. If your total budget is $60k, plan the project at $52k and keep the rest in reserve. If you don't spend it, it buys the better faucet at the end.

Cabinets and appliances swing a kitchen more than anything else. The spread between stock and custom cabinets alone can be $25k in the same layout. Lock those two decisions first and the rest of the budget gets much more predictable.

Get every quote in writing with line items. A single lump-sum number can't be compared to anything, and it can't be held to anything either. If a contractor won't itemize, that's a budgeting problem before it's a trust problem.

What Portland permits actually take

Permit timelines are the most common schedule surprise in this business, so here are the honest numbers we plan around.

Simple trade permits — water heater, panel swap, furnace — are usually issued over the counter or within a few days.

Kitchen and bath remodels that don't move structure typically need trade permits only: 1–2 weeks to have everything in hand.

Structural work and additions need full plan review. Budget 6–10 weeks from submission to issued permit, and longer if the reviewer asks for revised drawings — each round trip adds 2–3 weeks.

Historic and conservation districts add their own review on top. Our Irvington addition took nine weeks of district review before regular plan review even started. If your house is in one, ask us at the site visit — we'll know before we quote.

Two things we always do: file early, and never start regulated work without the permit in hand. An unpermitted addition can stall the sale of your house years later, and the fix costs far more than the wait ever did.

Five contractor red flags worth walking away from

We bid against a lot of other outfits. Most are decent. When a job goes wrong, it's usually after one of these warnings was ignored.

1. No CCB number on the paperwork. Oregon requires it on ads, bids, and contracts. You can check any license in 30 seconds at the CCB's site — status, bond, insurance, and complaint history. If the number is missing or "pending," stop.

2. A big cash deposit before any work. A materials deposit of 10–15% is normal. "Half up front, cash preferred" is not — it usually means your deposit funds someone else's unfinished job.

3. A verbal price. If the number isn't written down with line items, it isn't a price. It's an opening offer that grows.

4. "We don't need a permit for this." Sometimes true — but make the contractor say it in writing. If they won't, they know something they're not telling you.

5. Pressure to sign today. A real quote is good for weeks. Discounts that expire tonight exist to stop you from checking references. Take the weekend. Call the references. Any contractor doing honest work will still be there Monday.
